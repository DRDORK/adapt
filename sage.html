Internal demo · Sage

Meet Sage.
Music that knows how long you're working.

Sage is Freedom's adaptive focus music: a live soundtrack composed for every session, shaped to its exact length and resolving when it ends. Nothing else on the market does that: Endel adapts its soundscapes but not to a session's arc, and Brain.fm plays fixed tracks. Sage generates every note on the device in real time, so it costs nothing per minute and keeps playing through a full Freedom session.

Driving the demo
  1. Toggle Sage on. A preview sheet opens first: tap Hear 30 seconds (previews drop you into the middle of a composition, not the start) and switch styles while it plays. Each channel is in its own key.
  2. Continue to the simulated paywall and trial, then START THE SESSION.
  3. Under ⚙ Demo controls (below the phone), "run in 5 minutes" compresses the full arc so you can hear the whole shape. Uncheck it to run the real selected length.
  4. Try the "block all internet" checkbox: the music keeps playing. That's the point.

About the name

In the interface it's just Sage: one word, title case, the same green as Freedom's brand, calm as the herb it shares a name with. Underneath, it's an acronym: Session-Adaptive Generative Ensemble. The expansion is literally true, word by word. It adapts to the session, it generates rather than streams, and what plays is a true ensemble: drone, pads, bells, plucks, chords, and percussion, added and rested one by one as the arc moves.

Users never see the expansion. It lives a layer down, in pages like this one, for anyone who might enjoy knowing.

Shaped to the session, not the other way around

When a session starts, the music starts composing. Set 25 minutes and you get a 25-minute arc: a calm opening while you settle in, a long gradual build through deep focus, a lift for the final stretch, and a wind-down that ends exactly when you do. Set 3 hours and the same arc stretches to fit, with the on-ramp and wind-down staying tight while the deep-focus middle absorbs the length.

Intensity moves continuously, not in steps. Texture, brightness, rhythm, space, and harmony all ride this same curve, scaled to whatever length the session is.

No recorded track can do this. A file is one length forever: a 25-minute track can't stretch to a 50-minute session or tighten to a 15-minute one, so its build and resolution land wherever they land. Sage frees the arc from the file and composes the shape to fit, whether the session is 15 minutes or 3 hours.

One arc has a limit, though. Stretch it past a couple of hours and the deep-focus middle turns into a flat hour-long plateau, which the fatigue research argues against anyway. So sessions beyond roughly 2 to 3 hours, and open-ended or all-day sessions, switch to repeating waves: shorter cycles of build and ease, around 25 to 30 minutes each, with the final wave settling down when a known end approaches. The wave length is a tuning knob, and likely a personalization lever later (shorter waves for ADHD profiles, longer for deep-work veterans). The demo above plays the single arc; waves are specced for the production build.

You've been hearing “almost there” the whole time

Without knowing it. Freedom's original Focus Music was composed with a deliberate harmonic device: keep the harmony away from its home chord until the work is done. Unresolved harmony creates expectation, and expectation points you forward in time. You lean, just slightly, toward whatever comes next, in the music and in the work in front of you. That quiet not-finished-yet keeps you in the chair and the task moving, and the resolution at the end gives the session a real sense of completion.

Streaming AI music services can't reproduce this, because they don't control harmony. This engine can: we wrote it. Through the early session the progressions circle home without ever landing on it. As you climb toward the peak, the tonic starts appearing in passing inversions: partial resolutions, home glimpsed but not grounded. The final stretch lifts into high, bright voicings. And in the wind-down, for the first time in the whole session, the harmony lands fully on home in root position while the melodies do the same. Then you're done, and so is the music.

Built to be ignored

Every note is chosen in real time from rule systems with consonance guards, so the music evolves continuously without ever clashing or grabbing attention. No vocals, no drops, no sudden anything. Sustained tones are checked against everything still ringing before they're allowed to sound. Even the percussion is designed around this: snares with no crack, cymbals that swell instead of strike. Reduced auditory salience is the gating principle behind Freedom's original Focus Music, and here it's enforced by code.

And because nothing is a loop, no two sessions are ever the same. The drum patterns re-roll every eight bars, breakdowns arrive unpredictably, chord progressions re-draw from staged pools, and the windchimes only ring when a simulated breeze passes.

Grounded in research

The arc matches what research on flow and focus supports, not just the taste of the composer: music scaffolding attention, ramping it up, holding it steady without stealing it, and easing off before fatigue wins. The specific design choices and their evidence:

A phase-based arc, not a playlist. Flow is high task engagement, not relaxation, and it lives in a controllable arousal band between boredom and overload (van der Linden et al., 2021). The strongest focus-music experiment to date found instrumental, rhythmically clear, low-novelty music beat both "deep focus" playlists and pop during a demanding task (Orpella et al., 2025, PLOS ONE).
No lyrics, ever. Lyrics are the clearest avoid signal in the literature, especially for reading, writing, memory, and language-heavy work (Souza & Barbosa, 2023; Perham et al., 2024).
Nothing that grabs attention. Sage's consonance guards, soft percussion, and low harmonic novelty are an implementation of the same principle the research keeps landing on: mood lift must not come from attention-grabbing musical events.
Pulse and groove as primary levers. Amplitude modulation in background music measurably affects sustained attention, with individual differences that include ADHD traits (Woods et al., 2019). That backs the Lo-fi and Pulse channels today and per-user tuning later.
Night Rain is the creative mode. Moderate ambient noise improves creative cognition where heavy stimulation hurts it (Mehta, Zhu & Cheema, 2012).
Long sessions get waves, not escalation. Time-on-task fatigue is real and micro-recovery outperforms constant stimulation (Albulescu et al., 2022), which is why sessions past a couple of hours switch from one stretched arc to repeating waves.

None of this matters if someone presses play and doesn't like what they hear. The research can keep music from breaking focus; only musical judgment can make it worth listening to.

One refinement taken straight from this research is already in the engine: at the peak, rhythm and pulse clarity take the full climb while brightness and melodic density crest just below it. The final stretch keeps its lift, but it reads as drive rather than noise on tired attention.

Four channels, four characters

Ambient · D♭ major
Weightless pads and slow bells in the warmest key there is. At the peak the texture blooms: more shimmer, more bells, the drone's fifth swelling underneath.
Lo-fi · F major
Tape-warm electric piano and plucked melodies over a soft beat that assembles itself as you go deeper: hats, then shaker, then kick, bongos, backbeat, and a driving full kit at the peak.
Pulse · G major
A gentle synth pulse for steady momentum, with melodies locked to its grid.
Night Rain · A♭ major
Rain with real weather in it: layers that gust and recede, soft plucks, and distant windchimes that ring when a breeze passes.

Why on-device wins

It plays through a full block. Freedom's whole point is turning the internet off. A streaming music service dies the moment you block everything. This music is generated on the device, so it keeps playing through a block-everything session, on a plane, anywhere. It can't be interrupted by the very thing it's there to support.
Zero marginal cost. No per-minute licensing, no streaming infrastructure, no usage quotas, no metering. The economics of a streaming partner forced this to be a premium-only feature. On-device generation makes the packaging a product decision instead of a cost constraint.
Completely private. No audio or usage data leaves the device. Nothing to buffer, nothing to track, no cellular data used.
We own every note. No third-party licensing, no royalty questions, no sub-licensing tiers, no dependency on a vendor's roadmap. The musical rules are Freedom intellectual property, written in-house.
It extends a real product strength. Focus Sounds and composed Focus Music are among the most-loved parts of Freedom in reviews. This is the same musical DNA, made infinite and shaped to each session. And since it never repeats, there's nothing to wear out.
WHERE THIS GOES

From demo to instrument

THE NATIVE PORT: ONE CORE, EVERY PLATFORM

This demo runs on the Web Audio API, which ships in every browser and every iOS/Android webview today. Everything hard about this feature, the arc scheduler, the chord engine, the consonance rules, the arrangement logic, is platform-independent math. The plan is to keep it that way: a portable composition core with thin native audio shells per platform. On iOS that shell is AVAudioEngine, which is the first target because it solves the thing a webview can't promise: music that keeps generating with the screen locked and the phone in a pocket for a 3-hour session. On Android it's Oboe/AAudio (or a shared C++ core that covers both). And the web dashboard is the shortest path of all: this demo already IS the web build, running on the same Web Audio API the dashboard would use. The Mac and Windows apps follow from the same core in time. Write the instrument once; every platform plays it.

REAL INSTRUMENTS: THE SAMPLER

Native audio brings real sampler instruments: audio files mapped across the keyboard with velocity layers and envelopes (AVAudioUnitSampler on iOS, equivalent libraries elsewhere). Freedom owns thousands of hours of original stems from real recordings. A curated set of those, a piano sampled across its register, bowed strings, real percussion, turns this from a synth engine into an instrument that plays our own recordings, generatively recomposed, never repeating. No other focus product can have that sound, because no one else has the source material.

COMPOSING THE COMPOSER

The most interesting part of building this was the process: a musician describing what the music should do, in musical language, and the engine learning it as permanent rules. "Withhold the tonic until the end." "Let the tonic appear in inversions first, and save root position for the peak." "The plateau should rise." "Too much saturation early; keep the open clean and let the tape build toward the peak." "Bring the drums in part by part, and break it down so it never loops." "That drone clashes against a sustained second; make it resolve to the nearest chord tone, up or down." Each of those became code within minutes of being said, and the engine never forgets a rule once it's learned.

That working method scales. The next layers sit on the same foundation: motif and phrase structure so melodies develop ideas instead of wandering, voice-leading rules, modulation between keys across long sessions, session length mapping to real musical form rather than just an intensity curve, and independent tension curves for harmony, rhythm, and register. Every one of these is scheduling math plus musical judgment, and we have both in-house.
