Music that knows how long you're working

Adaptive Focus Music composes a live soundtrack for every Freedom session, shaped to its exact length. It builds while you work and resolves when you're done. Nothing streams: every note is generated on the device, so it costs nothing per minute and keeps playing through a full internet block.

Driving the demo
  1. Toggle Adaptive Focus Music on. You'll hit the paywall: that's part of the demo (it's a Premium feature). Tap through the free trial.
  2. Pick a Style, then START THE SESSION.
  3. Under ⚙ Demo controls (below the phone), "run in 5 minutes" compresses the full arc so you can hear the whole shape. Uncheck it to run the real selected length.
  4. Try the "block all internet" checkbox: the music keeps playing. That's the point.

Shaped to the session, not the other way around

When a session starts, the music starts composing. Set 25 minutes and you get a 25-minute arc: a calm opening while you settle in, a long gradual build through deep focus, a lift for the final stretch, and a wind-down that ends exactly when you do. Set 3 hours and the same arc stretches to fit, with the on-ramp and wind-down staying tight while the deep-focus middle absorbs the length.

The arc: settling in (low) → deep focus (a plateau that quietly rises) → final stretch (the peak) → wind-down (resolution). Intensity moves continuously, not in steps. Texture, brightness, rhythm, reverb, and harmony all ride the same curve.

This is something a fixed track can't do. Our composed Focus Music tracks are 25 minutes long because a file has to be some length. The craft in them was never the duration; it was the arc. This engine carries the arc forward and frees it from any fixed length.

The unresolved chord is doing the work

Freedom's original Focus Music was written by a concert pianist around one idea: keep the harmony away from its home chord until the work is done. That faint unresolved pull keeps you in the chair, and the resolution at the end gives the session a real sense of completion.

Streaming AI music services can't reproduce this. They don't control harmony. This engine does, because we wrote it. Through the early session the progressions circle home without ever landing on it. As you climb toward the peak, the tonic starts appearing in passing inversions: partial resolutions, home glimpsed but not grounded. The final stretch lifts into high, bright voicings. And in the wind-down, for the first time in the whole session, the harmony lands fully on home in root position while the melodies do the same. You've been hearing "almost there" the entire time without knowing it. Then you're done, and so is the music.

Built to be ignored

Every note is chosen in real time from rule systems with consonance guards, so the music evolves continuously but never clashes, never startles, and never grabs attention. No vocals, no drops, no sudden anything. Sustained tones are checked against everything still ringing before they're allowed to sound. Even the percussion is designed around this: snares with no crack, cymbals that swell instead of strike. Reduced auditory salience is the gating principle behind our composed tracks, and here it's enforced by code.

And because nothing is a loop, no two sessions are ever the same. The drum patterns re-roll every eight bars, breakdowns arrive unpredictably, chord progressions re-draw from staged pools, and the windchimes only ring when a simulated breeze passes.

Why on-device wins

It plays through a full block. Freedom's whole point is turning the internet off. A streaming music service dies the moment you block everything. This music is generated on the device, so it keeps playing through a block-everything session, on a plane, anywhere. It can't be interrupted by the very thing it's there to support.
Zero marginal cost. No per-minute licensing, no streaming infrastructure, no usage quotas, no metering. The economics of a streaming partner forced this to be a premium-only feature. On-device generation makes the packaging a product decision instead of a cost constraint.
Completely private. No audio or usage data leaves the device. Nothing to buffer, nothing to track, no cellular data used.
We own every note. No third-party licensing, no royalty questions, no sub-licensing tiers, no dependency on a vendor's roadmap. The musical rules are Freedom intellectual property, written in-house.
It extends a real product strength. Focus Sounds and composed Focus Music are among the most-loved parts of Freedom in reviews. This is the same musical DNA made infinite, session-aware, and impossible to outgrow.
WHERE THIS GOES

From demo to instrument

THE NATIVE PORT: AVAUDIOENGINE

This demo runs on the Web Audio API, which ships in every browser and every iOS/Android webview today. The production target is Apple's AVAudioEngine, the same node-graph architecture in native form. Everything hard about this feature, the arc scheduler, the chord engine, the consonance rules, the arrangement logic, is platform-independent math that ports as-is. What the native layer adds is the thing a webview can't promise: music that keeps generating with the screen locked and the phone in a pocket for an entire 3-hour session.

REAL INSTRUMENTS: THE SAMPLER

AVAudioEngine includes AVAudioUnitSampler, a true sampler instrument: audio files mapped across the keyboard with velocity layers and envelopes. Freedom owns thousands of hours of original stems from real recordings. A curated set of those, a piano sampled across its register, bowed strings, real percussion, turns this from a synth engine into an instrument that plays our own recordings, generatively recomposed, never repeating. No other focus product can have that sound, because no one else has the source material.

COMPOSING THE COMPOSER

The most interesting part of building this was the process: a musician describing what the music should do, in musical language, and the engine learning it as permanent rules. "Withhold the tonic until the end." "Let the tonic appear in inversions first." "The plateau should rise." "That drone clashes against a sustained second." Each of those became code within minutes of being said, and the engine never forgets a rule once it's learned.

That working method scales. Next layers on the same foundation: motif and phrase structure, so melodies develop ideas instead of wandering. Voice-leading rules. Modulation between keys across long sessions. Session length mapping to musical form, not just an intensity curve. Independent tension curves for harmony, rhythm, and register. Every one of these is scheduling math plus musical judgment, and we have both in-house.
